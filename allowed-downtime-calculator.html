What “allowed downtime” means

An SLA (Service Level Agreement) often promises a target like “99.9% uptime.” That number is not magic. It is a budget: how much time the service can be offline and still meet the promise.

Allowed downtime is that budget, written in time (seconds, minutes, or hours). If you stay inside the budget, you meet the uptime target. If you go over, your real uptime drops below the target.

This tool uses one clear formula. It matches the method on our SLA uptime → downtime reference and the same math used in the SLA Penalty Calculator.

Allowed downtime (minutes) = days × 24 × 60 × (1 − uptime / 100)

Achieved uptime (%) = 100 × (1 − downtime_minutes / (days × 24 × 60))

Why teams use this calculator

Managers often hear “three nines” or “four nines” and stop there. Engineers need the time budget. Finance needs the money risk. Allowed downtime is the bridge between the percentage and a real plan.

  • Set on-call and recovery goals that fit the SLA budget.
  • Compare vendors who sell different uptime numbers.
  • Check if last month’s outages still left you inside the contract target.
  • Explain risk to non-technical leaders in plain time units.

Worked examples

Example A — 99.9% over 30 days

Period = 30 × 24 × 60 = 43,200 minutes.

Allowed = 43,200 × (1 − 0.999) = 43,200 × 0.001 = 43.2 minutes.

That is 2,592 seconds, or 0.72 hours. One longer outage can use the whole month’s budget.

Example B — 99.99% over 30 days

Allowed = 43,200 × 0.0001 = 4.32 minutes (about 259 seconds).

Four nines sounds close to three nines. The budget is about 10× smaller.

Example C — Inverse check

You had 60 minutes of downtime in a 30-day month.

Uptime = 100 × (1 − 60 / 43,200) ≈ 99.861%.

That misses a 99.9% promise (budget was only 43.2 minutes).

Example D — Calendar month length matters

Same 99.9% target in a 31-day month: 31 × 24 × 60 = 44,640 minutes.

Allowed = 44,640 × 0.001 = 44.64 minutes — a bit more than the 30-day figure.

Always match the period your contract uses.

Common mistakes

  • Mixing day and month budgets. Daily and monthly numbers are not the same thing. A short daily outage can still burn a monthly budget if it happens often.
  • Ignoring exclusions. Contracts often exclude planned maintenance, customer-caused issues, or third-party networks. Allowed downtime is only the math budget — not always the credit rule.
  • Treating 99.9 and 99.99 as “almost the same.” One more nine cuts the downtime budget by about 10×. Cost and engineering effort rise fast.
  • Using 30 days when the SLA uses calendar months. February and 31-day months change the minutes. Use the period your provider defines.
  • Confusing uptime with user happiness. You can meet the SLA and still hurt customers if outages hit peak sales hours. Pair this tool with the downtime cost calculator.

How this fits with money and penalties

Allowed downtime answers “how much time can we lose?” It does not answer “how much money do we lose?” or “what credit do we owe?”

Frequently Asked Questions

What is allowed downtime?
Allowed downtime is how much time a service can be offline and still meet an uptime target. Formula: days × 24 × 60 × (1 − uptime/100).
How much downtime does 99.9% allow in 30 days?
43.2 minutes. That is 43,200 × 0.001 = 43.2 minutes (2,592 seconds, or 0.72 hours).
How do I get uptime % from downtime minutes?
Uptime% = 100 × (1 − downtime_minutes / (days × 24 × 60)). Example: 60 minutes down in 30 days ≈ 99.861% uptime.
Is a month always 30 days?
No. Many cloud SLAs use the real calendar month (28–31 days). Pick the period that matches your contract.
